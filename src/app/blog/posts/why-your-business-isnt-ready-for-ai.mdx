## Most AI Projects Fail. Here's Why.

The AI hype has made it seem like buying the right software solves your problems. Plug in ChatGPT, connect your CRM, and watch your productivity double.

It doesn't work that way.

I've worked with dozens of businesses trying to implement AI. The ones that fail follow a pattern: they implement AI on top of broken, undocumented, inconsistent foundations. The technology isn't the problem. The readiness is.

Here are the 5 things your business needs to fix before investing in AI.

---

## 1. Your Processes Are Not Documented

AI can't automate a process you can't describe. If you can't write down the exact steps for how a lead gets handled, how an invoice gets processed, or how a customer complaint gets resolved — AI can't reliably replicate it.

**The fix:**  
Spend one week documenting your top 5 most repetitive processes. Write them as numbered steps. Include decision points ("if X, then Y"). This documentation is the foundation that every AI implementation will be built on.

> "If you can't write it down, you can't automate it."

---

## 2. Your Data Is Messy, Incomplete, or Scattered

AI is only as good as the data it has access to. If your customer data is split across 3 different spreadsheets and a desk drawer of business cards, AI can't do anything meaningful with it.

**The fix:**  
Consolidate your core data into structured, consistent formats. Pick one CRM and actually use it. Standardize how you record customer information, transaction data, and project status. This is unglamorous work, but it's the single highest-leverage thing you can do before adopting AI.

---

## 3. Your Team Doesn't Understand What AI Can and Can't Do

AI implementations are often sabotaged by two extremes:
- **Over-trust**: Team members accept AI output without checking it, leading to errors being shipped
- **Under-trust**: Team members ignore AI tools entirely because they had one bad experience

Both are dangerous. What you need is calibrated trust — understanding which tasks AI reliably handles and which it doesn't.

**The fix:**  
Run a 2-hour AI literacy workshop with your team. Cover: what AI is great at (drafting, summarizing, pattern recognition), what it's bad at (novel reasoning, recent events, emotional nuance), and how to verify AI output.

---

## 4. You Don't Have an AI Champion

AI adoption fails without internal momentum. If everyone expects the technology to implement itself, nothing happens. You need one person who is genuinely excited about AI, has the authority to run experiments, and will push the team to integrate new tools.

**The fix:**  
Identify your AI champion. This isn't necessarily your most technical person — it's the person who's most curious, most willing to experiment, and most likely to influence others. Give them dedicated time (2-3 hours/week) to explore AI tools and report findings.

---

## 5. You're Trying to Solve the Wrong Problems First

Businesses often try to automate their most complex, exception-heavy processes first because those are the most painful. But those are exactly the wrong places to start.

The right starting point is: **high-frequency, low-exception, rule-based tasks**.

- Does every customer inquiry require a completely custom response? → Hard to automate first
- Does every sales lead need to be added to your CRM? → Perfect first automation

**The fix:**  
List your 20 most frequent tasks. Score them 1-5 on: frequency, consistency (how often does it follow the same pattern?), and time cost. The tasks with the highest combined score are your automation starting points.

---

## After You've Fixed These 5 Things

Once you have documented processes, clean data, an AI-literate team, an internal champion, and have identified the right starting points — you are genuinely ready to implement AI.

And at that point, the returns are significant. Not "maybe 10% more productive" significant. More like "we just replaced 3 hours of daily manual work with a 5-minute review" significant.

The work that most businesses skip — the boring groundwork — is exactly what separates successful AI implementations from expensive mistakes.
